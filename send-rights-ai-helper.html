Why AI?

There is not a single professional who has the time to understand the complexities and lengths you have taken to help your child. Not one person who can sit with you long enough to uncover all the things you already know — buried under years of meetings, emails, assessments, and crisis — and help you put it all out clearly.

There is no one to debug what you have done. To go back through everything that has happened and find the flaws in the system you have encountered. No one to help you unpick it all, in your own time, so you can advocate better for yourself and your child.

This tool does that. It helps you summarise everything — your child’s needs, your timeline, your experiences — in a way that is impossible to do on your own, because none of us are lawyers, healthcare professionals, and education specialists all at once. And here is the truth: there is not a single professional who knows all of these things either. No one joins the dots between education, health, and social care — except you, the parent, holding it all together.

AI can. It can sit with you at 2am when IPSEA is closed and SENDIAS has a waiting list. It can explain things three different ways without losing patience. It can take everything you tell it and help you see the full picture — the legal rights, the duties that have been breached, the remedies you are entitled to — and help you put that into words that get heard.

AI is not perfect. It can make mistakes. It is not a lawyer. This tool says that clearly, and it tells you where to go for proper legal advice. But for a parent who does not even know they have rights, an AI that can say “the council has a legal duty to educate your child, and here is how you hold them to it” is transformative.

This is not about replacing human support. It is about helping you join the dots to get what you and your family are legally entitled to.

Data we may collect (only with your consent)

At the end of your conversation, the AI may offer to collect two types of data. Both are entirely optional. You can say no to either or both. Your help from the tool is never conditional on sharing data.

1. Feedback data

What: Anonymised observations about what worked, what was confusing, and what was missing in the tool. This is compiled by the AI from your conversation — you are shown exactly what will be submitted and can edit or remove anything before it goes anywhere.

Why: To improve the tool for future families.

What is stored: Your region (local authority area — not your address), your situation type (e.g. “child out of school”), and the feedback summary.

What is NOT stored: Your name, email address, specific dates, school names, or anything that could identify you or your child.

Can I withdraw it? Feedback data has no email attached — it is truly anonymous. Once submitted, it cannot be linked back to you, so it cannot be withdrawn. You see and approve everything before submission.

2. Lived experience data

What: An anonymised summary of your family’s journey through the SEND system — compiled from what you already shared in the conversation. No additional interview. You see exactly what will be submitted and can edit or remove anything.

Why this matters

The government’s own SEND reform green paper acknowledged that the current system is failing children and families. But one group is consistently missing from the evidence base: the SEND betweeners — children who are not “severe enough” for an EHCP but whose needs are not being met by mainstream provision. These are the children who mask, who cope until they can’t, who fall out of school with no alternative in place. Their families’ experiences are some of the most complex and the least documented.

Good policy should be built on user-centred design — understanding the real experiences of the people a system is supposed to serve, not just the professionals who run it. If the government genuinely wants to fix SEND, it needs to hear from the families living through it: the gaps between services, the referral loops, the years of crisis before anyone listens. Without that evidence, reform risks being another set of plasters holding a broken system together rather than actually solving the problems.

It would be a shame for families to work through such rich, painful lived experience in conversation with this tool and for that insight to be lost. If you choose to share your anonymised journey, it could contribute to:

  • Evidence for policy reform — demonstrating the real impact of system failures on families, particularly SEND betweeners
  • Academic research — into the specific barriers neurodivergent children face in accessing education and health provision that meets their needs
  • Service redesign — helping build systems that actually work for the families they are supposed to support

Any future use of lived experience data — whether for research, policy, or advocacy — will require separate, specific consent. We will contact you at the email address you provided to ask. You can always say no.

What is stored: Your email address, your region (local authority area), your situation type, and the anonymised journey summary.

Can I withdraw it? Yes — see below.

How your data is stored

If you provide lived experience data, we store your anonymised journey and your email address together so that we can contact you for consent if a specific use arises, and so that you can request deletion.

Data is stored in Supabase, which provides encryption at rest and is hosted on infrastructure within the EU/UK.

Only Nic Whippey has access to the data. It is not shared with anyone unless you give specific, separate consent.

Lawful basis

Consent (Article 6(1)(a), UK GDPR). You actively choose to share data. You can withdraw that consent at any time.

How to withdraw your data

To withdraw your lived experience data:

  1. Email from the same email address you provided when you shared your data
  2. We find your record, delete your lived experience data, and delete your email address from our database
  3. We confirm the deletion to you by email

Withdrawal is as easy as giving consent. Your data is fully deleted, not just hidden.

How long we keep your data

Feedback data: Retained indefinitely (it is anonymous and cannot be linked to you).

Lived experience data: Retained until you withdraw it, or until this project ends — whichever comes first. If the project ends, all data will be deleted and you will be notified at the email address you provided.

Email address: Retained only as long as the corresponding lived experience data exists. Deleted when you withdraw, or when the project ends.

Your rights under UK GDPR

You have the right to:

  • Access — ask what data we hold about you
  • Rectification — ask us to correct inaccurate data
  • Erasure — ask us to delete your data (see withdrawal process above)
  • Withdraw consent — at any time, without affecting the help you received
  • Complain — to the Information Commissioner’s Office if you believe your data has been mishandled

Children’s data

We do not collect data directly from children. All data is provided by parents or carers. The anonymisation process removes all information that could identify a child — names, school names, specific dates, and any identifying combinations.
