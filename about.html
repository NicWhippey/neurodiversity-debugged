Why this exists

My name is Nic Whippey. I am a parent of a neurodivergent child in England, and for years I watched the systems that were supposed to help my family fail us — slowly, bureaucratically, and completely.

My child was struggling. School said he was fine. He was not fine. He was masking — holding everything together in the classroom at enormous cost, then falling apart the moment he got home. I knew something was wrong long before any professional agreed with me. By the time they did, he was in crisis.

I learned the SEND system the hard way: from a phone, lying in bed during burnout, because nobody was helping and I had to help myself. I taught myself education law, SEND legislation, neurodevelopmental research, and complaints processes — not because I wanted to, but because the system left me no choice. I became an expert in my own child’s rights because no professional told me what those rights were.

Along the way I discovered something that made me angry: the law was on my side the whole time. The duties were clear. The rights existed. The information was publicly available. But the bridge between the law and the families it is supposed to protect was missing. No one told me my child did not need a diagnosis to have special educational needs. No one told me the council had a legal duty to educate my child from day 15 of absence. No one told me I could request an EHC needs assessment without the school’s permission. I had to find every single piece of it myself.

And I am one of the lucky ones. I have a background in neuroscience, which makes it easier for me to digest what is going on with my own and my child’s neurodiversity — the biology, the mechanisms, the reasons behind the struggles. I am neurodivergent myself, which means I can hyperfocus on a problem until I understand it completely. And I am a Senior Research Software Engineer, which means I know how to build things. I had the words, the knowledge, and the energy — eventually — to fight. Most parents do not. Most parents are so exhausted, so blamed, so gaslit by the system that they give up. Their children fall through the gap. Not because the law does not protect them, but because nobody ever told them it did.

That is why I built Neurodiversity Debugged.

What “Debugged” means

“Debugged” is a play on words — because I am a software engineer, and because that is exactly what I am doing.

I am debugging the system: the education, health care, and social care structures that are supposed to help neurodivergent families but are riddled with failures. The code is there — the law exists, the duties are clear, the rights are written down — but the implementation is broken. Schools that cannot identify masking children. Councils that refuse to assess because the child is “fine in school.” Health pathways that force you to choose between an ADHD assessment and an autism assessment when your child has both. Referral loops that send you in circles until you give up.

And I am debugging the brain and body — using my neuroscience background to understand the mechanisms, the causes, the reasons my child and I malfunction in societal norms like mainstream school. Not because we are broken, but because the environments we are expected to survive in were never designed for brains like ours.

Parents spend years debugging a system that should have worked the first time. They become the project managers of their own child’s survival, holding together education, health, and social care that were supposed to be joined up but never are.

These tools help you find the bug, name it, and file the report.

What I am building

I am a developer. AI is a tool I use daily in my work, and prompt engineering is always on my periphery. But I also use AI for something more personal: to help me write — my therapy, published on Medium — and to help me understand what is going on. Why are we in crisis? What does the law actually say? What is the biology behind my child’s experience? Anything that seems relevant to getting the next step in helping my family, when the system feels like it is actively blocking me.

What I learn, I turn into AI tools to help other families. And what I live through, I write about — to give other parents a voice, understanding, and the validation that comes from knowing someone else has been through the same thing. I bear my soul for all to see, because silence helps nobody.

Neurodiversity Debugged takes everything I have learned about the SEND system and makes it available to every family, for free, 24 hours a day, in a conversation that is patient, warm, and trauma-informed. It can listen to a parent’s story without rushing, explain their legal rights in plain English, and write a formal complaint letter that the council cannot ignore.

The SEND Rights AI Helper is currently in testing and will be the first tool to launch. The same approach — trauma-informed AI, led by lived experience, backed by law — can work for other systems that fail neurodivergent families: mental health, employment, benefits, housing.
